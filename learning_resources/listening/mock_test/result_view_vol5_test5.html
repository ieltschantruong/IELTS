SECTION 4

Lecturer: Welcome to today's lecture, where we'll be discussing the fascinating intersection of Economics and Trust. Trust is often seen merely as a social construct, but it is a fundamental pillar of economic exchange. In many daily transactions, rather than relying strictly on contracts, trust relies heavily on the stranger's kindness [Q31]. We assume the other party will act fairly and predictably.

Furthermore, extensive macroeconomic research suggests that a proper understanding and implementation of trust systems can actually reduce poverty [Q32] by significantly lowering transaction costs and encouraging foreign investment and trade.

So, how do we measure this elusive concept of trust? One fascinating method looks at biology and psychology, specifically evaluating people's ability of recognizing a person's face [Q33]. Studies show that the quicker and more accurately we process facial cues, the better we are at assessing trustworthiness.

Chemically, trust is inextricably linked to a hormone called oxytocin. Oxytocin will be released in the brain when interaction [Q34] occurs between individuals in a positive, cooperative context.

Let's look at some classic economic experiments involving two participants to demonstrate this. In the standard "trust game," the first participant decides whether to lend a sum of money or not. Interestingly, the rules dictate that the second participant has no legal obligation [Q35] to return the money. This setup proves that trust involves a significant risk [Q36], so it is conditional upon the expected behavior and perceived fairness of the borrower.

Therefore trust is conditional, people should not take it for granted. Oxytocin does not only exist in human beings, animals also have this gift and it can be seen when they have trust [Q37] within their social groups and cooperation with each other. Oxytocin in monkeys, for example, allows them to work together to find food and to fight against enemies.

Now, applying this to game theory, let's consider the Nash Equilibrium. In the Nash Equilibrium's Game Theory, the first participant will make the best decision he can with no expectation [Q38] that the second participant would change his decision. The second participant will do the same, so the outcome depends on the decision of the other person. This game is similar to the one we just talked about.

The oxytocin level doesn't stay the same in a person's body. Studies have found that people who are under a lot of stress would have higher [Q39] levels of oxytocin. Oxytocin causes a substantial increase in trust among humans, thereby greatly increasing the benefits from social interactions. It's also noted that the effect of oxytocin on trust is not due to a general increase in the readiness to bear risks, on the contrary, oxytocin specifically affects an individual's willingness to accept social risks arising through interpersonal interactions.

To sum up, oxytocin is like a kind of social glue [Q40] that is helpful in social bonding. Further research is needed to explore oxytocin and trust and how this hormone can be used to strengthen our trust in others and even treat mental disorders that specifically relate to deficiencies in social behavior.
